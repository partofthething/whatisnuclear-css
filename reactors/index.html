Types of Nuclear Reactors

By Nick Touran, September 15, 2010.

There are very many types of nuclear reactors, each with its own capabilities, benefits, and shortcomings. A very few possible types of nuclear reactors have been built, and many more have been conceived of and at least partially analyzed. Here we discuss a few popular reactor types (both real and conceptual).

Almost all nuclear reactors have at least two things in common. They all have fuel, from which we hope to extract energy and they all have coolant, which we use to carry the energy from the fuel to where we'd like to use it (typically to an electric generator or a propeller). To get a short list of the reactor types, we can take a look at all the so-far identified useful fuels and coolants.

  • Fuel nuclides: Uranium-235, Plutonium-239 (produced from Uranium-238), Uranium-233 (produced from Thorium)
  • Fuel forms: Ceramic pellets, ceramic pebbles, metallic plates, metallic pins, liquid salt, aqueous
  • Coolants: Pressurized water, boiling water, heavy water, liquid metal, liquid salt, helium, lithium, other gases

There are many overlapping ways to organize reactor types. We’ll try it by coolant.

Light Water Reactors (LWRs)

LWRs are by far the most common type of nuclear reactor in the world. Light water is just regular old water. [Read more]

CANDU Reactors

Canadian Deuterium-Uranium reactors use heavy water as a coolant. Heavy water is processed water that only contains Deuterium (hydrogen with an extra neutron). There are many of these in Canada. [Read more]

Liquid-Metal Cooled Fast Reactors (LMFRs)

Several of these reactors operate. They have special capabilities above what LWRs can do and have received much attention throughout the years (DOE’s IFR program, TerraPower Traveling Wave Reactors, etc.). [Read more]

High Temperature Gas-Cooled Reactors (HTGRs)

These reactors use special materials that can withstand very high temperatures. At such temperature, conversion of heat to electricity is extremely efficient. Also, many chemical processes can use the waste heat from these reactors. You could use these to extract shale oil, produce hydrogen from water for cars, and more!. [Read more]

Molten Salt Reactors (MSRs)

There reactors use a liquid fuel! This presents many amazing capabilities and opportunities (as well as some downsides). There is currently a large group of energetic activists promoting these types of reactors. [Read more]

Did you know?

The first electricity produced by a nuclear reactor came from a liquid-metal cooled fast breeder reactor called EBR-I [anl.gov] in Arco, Idaho. The plant is now a very cool museum. Check it out next time you drive that way.
