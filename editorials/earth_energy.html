The classification of all common energy sources.

There are only 3 energy sources.

By Nick Touran, June 2007

I've done a lot of thinking about the big picture of energy, and I've come to the conclusion that all energy we use can be grouped into three categories, of which only one is significant in daily life.

Nuclear Fusion (Solar Power)

The energy that comes from the sun via nuclear fusion dominates our energy supplies.

  • Fossil Fuels - Assuming that the organic origin theory of fossil fuels is true and thus that these hydrocarbons are the remnants of decaying prehistoric plants and animals, it is easy to conclude that solar power is at work. The animals ate the plants, and the plants used photosynthesis to create sugars from CO2. Those solar-powered sugars eventually have become our oil, coal, gasoline, diesel, kerosene, etc.
  • Food - Along the same lines as the fossil fuels, the base of our modern food chain is photosynthesis in plants. This means that every time you walk, think, dance, or create, you can thank solar nuclear fusion for providing you with the means to do so. The ancient sun worshipers had the right idea!
  • Solar power - Duh. Bypassing photosynthesis, these technologies convert the energy from the sun into electricity most often by exciting electrons in semiconductors.
  • Wind energy - Wind? Yes that's right. Where does wind come from? It comes from pressure differences, which are caused by weather patterns, which are caused by the interaction of the atmosphere and seas, which are unevenly heated by the sun!
  • Hydroelectric energy - Often people chalk this up to gravity. But gravity is a conservative force, like the magnetic force, and it will not produce energy on its own. Granted, hydroelectric power would not work without gravity. But the real energy enters the system when the water is lifted up in the first place. We've all seen the graphics of the water cycle. Water gets to the top of rivers by evaporating into the air and then raining down. This evaporation is caused by none other than solar heat.

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal Energy is not as directly related to the sun. Much of the energy comes from the radioactive decay of natural nuclides such as Thorium and Uranium in the makeup of the Earth.

Tidal Energy

When the tides come in and out, and where we have been smart enough to set up tidal turbines, we are extracting energy from the orbiting Earth-Moon 2-body system. The gravitational pull of the moon causes the ebb and flow of the tides. Each day, tidal friction is extracted from the Earth-Moon system. Maybe someday, we'll have to worry about releasing the moon from orbit.
