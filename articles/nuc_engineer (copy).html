Nuclear Engineers

Nuclear engineers apply knowledge of atomic nuclei to design, build, and operate special equipment that is useful to humanity. These applications fall into several broad categories, including fission power, radiation detection, nuclear medicine, nuclear materials, and plasma science. These categories overlap all the time, since nuclear materials knowledge is required for all applications, nuclear medicine and fission power depend on accurate radiation detection, and so on.

Fission Power

Nuclear engineers working in fission support power-producing nuclear reactors in one way or another. Many work for vendors like GE or Westinghouse who originally built the power plants. They figure out how to load the new fuel into the reactor at the next refueling. These people often support many reactors. Others work at the utilities - the actual owners of the power plants such as NRG and Constellation. They would do similar work specifically on one or two plants. They perform safety analyses and try to keep the plant operating as well as possible.

Outside of the utility industry, fission people might work for the nuclear navy building reactors to power submarines and aircraft carriers. They might go to a national lab like Los Alamos, Argonne, Sandia, or Idaho National Lab and design advanced reactors such as sodium-cooled fast reactors (SFR) or very high temperature reactors (VHTR). Others work for the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) in Maryland, where they inspect all nuclear activities in the country and keep things operating safely.

Medical and Health Physics

Radiation has many applications in the medical fields. Targeting tumorous tissue with radiation beams to fight cancer is a common procedure. Since radioactive isotopes emit energetic particles, you can place them in the body and see where the material goes. This is useful for tracing and diagnosing diseases. Nuclear engineers who understand how radiation interacts with tissues are vital to these useful applications.

Plasma science

Engineers working with plamas typically either direct plasmas into materials to make powerful computer chips, design plasma space systems that will be needed to propel manned missions to deeper space, or work on getting fusion power (the holy grail of energy) into human"s control.

Radiation Detection

Experts in radiation detectors can find jobs in many fields. Most recently, increased national security produced lots of demand for border and port scanning devices. Using radiation, we can get a read-out of many of the materials contained in a truck or a shipping container without even opening it. The oil industry uses radiation detectors to detect where oil fields are when prospecting. Hydrogen storage people use neutron scattering to characterize materials best suited for fuel cells. Geologists use radioactive dating to determine the age of rocks and even the Earth.

Nuclear Materials

Materials people deal with the interaction of radiation with materials. This is a vast field with applications ranging from computer chip manufacturing to space elevators.

  • Controlling a chain-reaction of splitting large atoms in a reactor...
    • to spin a turbine to make electricity for buildings.
    • to spin a turbine to make electricity for submarines and aircraft carriers.
    • to produce radioactive materials used in industry and medicine.
  • Using radioactive material to investigate things.
    • Imaging objects such as human organs (for medicine) or shipping containers (for homeland security).
    • Monitoring the amount of high-sulfur coal that is burned in a coal plant.
    • Searching for oil.
    • Dating dinosaur bones.
    • Identifying the precise contents of any object (for instance, to learn if it is from outer space or not).
  • Using radioactive material as a battery that lasts over 200 years in space probes or military equipment.
  • Attempting to harness the power of the sun (sort of like Doc Ock) in a viable fusion reactor.
  • Many other things.

The industries listed in table 1 are very broad, being defined by the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Most nuclear engineers specialize in one of the following more specific areas.

  • Fission technology - work on systems related to nuclear reactors.
  • Nuclear materials - study radiation effects on materials such as metals, semiconductors, etc.
  • Nuclear detection - design devices capable of reading all sorts of radiation fields.
  • Plasmas - study applications of the 4th state of matter. These studies focus on fusion power, semiconductor processing, and electromagnetic weaponry
  • Radiological science - provide medical imaging, diagnostics, and radiation cancer treatment.
