Fast Reactors

By Nick Touran, July 2009.

What is a fast reactor?

Nuclear reactors can be classified into either fast reactors or thermal reactors. An in-between reactor would be called epithermal. But what does this mean? What is so fast about fast reactors? It’s the neutrons. On average, neutrons in a fast reactor move around much more quickly than those in a thermal reactor, which are moving at thermal energies -- moving at the same speed that atoms vibrate at a given temperature. So a fast reactor is simply a nuclear reactor designed to keep the neutrons moving quickly. If you want numbers, your average thermal neutron moves around at about 2200 m/s while a fast neutron might be cruising well above 9 million m/s, which is about 3% of the speed of light.

What’s the big deal with fast reactors?

Understanding the answer requires a very basic understanding of nuclear physics. See below for such details if you are curious. Otherwise, the main points are summarized as pros and cons below.

Pros

  • fast reactors have more neutrons to go around, so many can be used to breed new fuel, vastly enhancing the sustainability of nuclear power.
  • fast reactors are capable of destroying the nastiest kinds of nuclear waste, transforming it to waste that decays to harmlessness in centuries rather than hundreds of millennia.
  • Fast reactors can employ metallic fuel rather than oxides. Since metal expands rapidly with heat, fuel in a fast reactor unexpectedly heating up will naturally move away from itself, no matter what the human operators do wrong. This enhances the safety of these reactors significantly.

Cons

  • Time scales in fast reactors are typically faster than those in thermal reactors (mostly because there are less delayed neutrons in fast reactors). Thus the reactor can possibly go through transients (bad) faster than thermal reactors.
  • Bubbles in fast reactor coolant can cause the reactor to heat up rather than cool down. This positive feedback is scary (but manageable!).
  • To keep the neutrons moving quickly, fast reactors require exotic coolants derived from heavy atoms. The most common coolant is liquid sodium, which is well known but highly reactive with air and water. Another is liquid lead-bismuth eutectic, which isn’t the most pleasant material either. These bizarre materials require extra care and quality in many systems, possibly bringing costs up.

Technical Details

First, go read our moderation page for a second and come back when you understand that neutrons emerge from fission reactions at high speeds and that we typically like to slow them down to thermal energies in order to increase their chances of continuing the chain reaction.

Knowing that, however, now know that splitting atoms is not the only thing neutrons do. In nuclides such as Uranium-238, thermal neutrons are readily absorbed without causing a fission -- resulting in what we call a capture. The probability for capture also increases dramatically at thermal energies. We have measured through experiments that the chances that a neutron will cause a capture rather than a fission in Uranium-238 are high at thermal energies and become smaller at faster energies. Take a look at these two graphs showing the probability of capture and the probability of fission as a function of neutron energy for U-235 and U-238.

Recalling that standard (thermal) reactor fuel is 4% U-235 and 96% U-238, you can see that with this, U-238 could actually release energy through fission, just like its less-common isotope U-235 if the neutrons were fast enough. Notice that depending on the enrichment, our fuel’s properties will be a mixture of these two plots. Focusing on the thermal energies (less than 10 eV), you can see that as enrichment goes up, the overall probability of fission becomes higher than the probability of capture (plus a few other loss mechanisms like leakage out of the reactor)

Nuclear reactors need to cool fuel as it undergoes a fission chain reaction. Water is the standard coolant of other power plants for various reasons (it plays well with steam turbines, is clean, is plentiful, has decent thermal properties, etc.), so it was an obvious candidate for nuclear reactors. Luckily, it doesn’t eat up too many neutrons through capture, so it turned out to be a feasable choice.

Did you know?

If you make a fast reactor flat like a pancake, it’s considered safer than taller reactors. The increased loss of neutrons out the top and bottom counteract positive feedback caused by spectral hardening in the low capture-to-fission regime.
