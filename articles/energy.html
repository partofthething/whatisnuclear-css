What is energy?

September 1, 2008

Don‘t be ashamed if you aren‘t completely sure what energy really is. It‘s one of those abstract concepts that is difficult to define. We‘ve even heard microbiology Ph.D students admit that they were a little confused. Here‘s a general overview.

Energy is the capability to produce effects. Every single effect you have ever seen required energy. Cars driving, stoves heating, lights flashing, even falling in love all need a certain amount of energy to happen. We use energy by first finding places where it has been stored and then by cleverly tapping into them.

Very often, we find energy stored in chemical bonds. Plants store energy from the sun in starches and sugars to give us living things energy through the food chain. Plants that died long ago and became buried have become fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas. The energy stored (again from the sun) way back then is still in those chemical bonds, and we can burn them as fuel for our homes, cars, and to charge our cell phones. When the sun shines on a lake or ocean, water stores energy by heating up. Some of it evaporates and goes high into the mountains, where it then rains down. We can intercept the water on its way back to the ocean with a hydroelectric dam and use the energy in our favorite form: electricity.


Here‘s a figure of the water cycle using energy from the sun to make electricity with a hydroelectric dam.

Almost all the energy that we use on Earth comes from the sun. Wind, solar, hydro, and fossil fuels are all almost directly related to the sun. Exceptions include tidal energy, which comes from energy stored in the 2-body gravitational system made by the Earth and Moon. Geothermal energy is part radioactive decay-heat and part residual heat energy from the formation of the planet. Nuclear energy is stored in the bonds between protons and neutrons in the centers of atoms - the nucleus (compare to chemical bonds between electrons in molecules).

What about power?

Power is a rate of energy transfer. If you had a bag that held 5 units of energy and you let it out of the bag in 5 seconds, you would have been running your bag at a power of 1 unit of energy per second. Less abstractly, energy is usually measured in Joules, so if you had 5 Joules of energy saved up in a battery, and you ran your flashlight for 5 seconds before the energy was all out, your flashlight was running at (and using) 1 Joule per second, or 1 Watt.

Here at whatisnuclear.com, we use energy and power interchangeably. Nuclear energy is sitting in our fuel rods being used up in our power plants. The size of the plant determines the actual power (again, the speed at which the energy is used up).
